Using Kafka for risk and machine learning

Three risk problems use different time horizons. Kafka moves events; the business problem determines whether scoring is batch or real time.

Example 1 · real-time session intelligence

Geospatial session risk

Business problem: identify unusual stops or route patterns early without treating one location point as proof of fraud.

1 · ProducersLocation, trip state, event time
→
2 · Kafka input topicObservations keyed by trip or session
→
3 · Stateful consumerMaintains a recent bounded window
→
4 · ClassifierRules first; machine learning when labels exist
→
5 · Kafka output topicAssessment type, score, reasons, time
→
6 · DecisionsReview, notification, and analytics
KafkaTransports durable observations and classified results.
Model timingReal-time rules or inference over a short state window.
OutputExplainable session-level risk signal, not a verdict.
DecisionA policy or reviewer decides whether to notify or investigate.
Why streaming matters: order and event time provide the context that an isolated location point cannot provide.
Boundary: traffic, location error, a store visit, or waiting may explain the same pattern. The classifier estimates risk, not intent.

Example 2 · batch model with real-time reuse

Historical account risk

Business problem: use long-term behavior to prioritize accounts with persistent marketplace-integrity risk.

1 · Historical sourcesOrders, disputes, devices, patterns
→
2 · Batch storeGoverned history and labels
→
3 · Batch model jobScheduled features, training, and scoring
→
4 · Score storeLatest account score and explanation
→
5 · Optional stream useA consumer reads the latest score as context
→
6 · Risk reviewPrioritize, investigate, decide
KafkaOptional in the core batch path; useful for feeding history or enriching live decisions.
Model timingBatch training and batch scoring on a schedule.
OutputAccount-level risk score reflecting long-term behavior.
DecisionReviewer prioritization; deactivation requires explicit policy and evidence.
Hybrid pattern: a real-time consumer may read the latest batch score as context, but that does not make the batch model itself real time.

Example 3 · real-time security response

Account takeover (ATO) detection

Business problem: detect a sudden compromise quickly enough to reduce harm while protecting the legitimate account owner.

1 · Event producersLogin, multi-factor authentication, device, card events
→
2 · Kafka input topicsDurable security-event streams
→
3 · ATO consumerBuilds online features from recent events and history
→
4 · Real-time scoringAccount takeover risk score and reasons
→
5 · Kafka output topicAssessment, evidence time, model version
→
6 · Protective responseReview, verify, contain
KafkaCarries events that drive detection and the typed assessment downstream.
Model timingBatch training; real-time inference when a security event arrives.
OutputAccount takeover risk score, reason codes, and evidence timestamps.
DecisionHuman review first; bounded protective action only under approved policy.
Why streaming matters: success is measured by time from signal to detection to resolution, not only by model accuracy.
Important distinction: the account owner may be the victim. Account takeover is not the same as long-term account risk.
